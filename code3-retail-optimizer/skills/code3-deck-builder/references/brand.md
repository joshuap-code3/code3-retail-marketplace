# Code3 Brand Reference

Everything in this file comes from the Code3 Brand Book (Dec 2023) and patterns observed in real production decks (QBRs, All Hands, capabilities). Treat it as the source of truth for voice and visual choices when the template doesn't dictate them directly.

## Voice & tone

Code3's voice is **casual but professional, full of personality, and confident without being cocky.** It sits between a consultancy tone (too dry) and a scrappy agency tone (too loose). Key traits:

- **Direct and to-the-point.** Say the thing. No long windups.
- **Confident: "we are the experts".** Make claims, don't hedge. Don't say "we believe" or "it appears" — just say what's true.
- **Always answer "so what?"** Every chart, every insight, every bullet should explicitly tell the reader why it matters to their business. The QBR analysis showed this is mandatory: no context-only slides exist.
- **Layman's terms, not jargon-for-jargon's-sake.** When you use industry language (ROAS, NTB, DPVR, LALs), make sure the surrounding copy grounds it.
- **Interject personality.** Hot takes, opinions, "our POV is…" is welcome. But don't be so casual that the reader can't take it seriously. No swearing, no slang.
- **Educational with swagger.** "Here's how it works, and here's what that means for you."
- **It's personal.** Code3 takes client growth personally — that theme recurs. Language like "your brand," "your customers," "we went and…" keeps it direct.

### Voice examples — client-facing

Good (from real Code3 decks):
- "Amlactin is seeing YoY growth driven by creator-led social."
- "The Hook → Demo → Reveal arc keeps viewers through the payoff."
- "Shifting Q1 strategy towards: Social-forward, creator-led content, with an emphasis on eRetail."
- "We're always learning — ↑Tailwinds / ↓Headwinds."
- "Continued scaling and refining the Q4 strategy overhaul."

Bad (fix to be more Code3):
- ❌ "We are pleased to present the Q1 performance review." → ✅ "Q1 in review."
- ❌ "Various factors contributed to performance trends." → ✅ "Three things drove Q1 growth: creator content, eRetail, and the new LALs."
- ❌ "Consider implementing the following recommendations." → ✅ "Next up: three bets for Q2."
- ❌ "Please refer to the appendix for detailed methodology." → ✅ "Methodology in the appendix."

### Voice — internal vs. external

From the All Hands analysis, the internal voice is **rawer and more operational**: names specific clients and dollar impact, uses bold normative language ("Zero Tolerance", "Every 1:1 must touch on ALL accounts"), occasionally includes asides ("*gestures wildly*"). It acknowledges gaps openly ("Where We Need to Be Better").

Client-facing decks keep the direct, confident tone but **remove** self-deprecation, in-jokes, and vulnerability framing. They stay professionally warm and strategically confident. Names Code3's own clients only with permission; never names competitor clients by name.

If the user doesn't specify internal vs. external, **assume external/client-facing** — that's the stated scope of this skill.

## Colors

The Code3 palette is tightly restricted. The template's slides use these colors correctly — you don't need to set them — but use this reference when writing copy that references colors, when picking between slide variants, or if you ever need to hand-color something.

**Primary (foundation):**
- White `#FFFFFF`
- Grey `#F7F7F7` (use when white needs a little separation or hierarchy)
- Black `#201E20` (slightly softer than pure black)

**Secondary (support in print/presentations; CTA colors in digital):**
- Green `#E7F3AC` (light/"lime green" per the template — dominant accent)
- Blue `#2985FD`

**Accent (sparingly):**
- Red `#F05024` — use as an accent only, not a fill.

**Additional palette values** seen in the template (from its internal "color values" reference): `#2E5C00` (deep green), `#C5E525` (mid green), `#ABC9F1` (soft blue), `#004CAF` (deep blue), `#9C2200` (deep red), `#F2BBAC` (salmon).

**60/30/10 rule.** Compositions use 60% dominant color, 30% secondary, 10% accent. Most slides lead with white or black; the other colors support.

## Typography

**Primary typeface: Barlow.** All slide typography uses Barlow. The template has Barlow embedded; don't swap to a different font.

- **Barlow Light** — display applications (big presentation titles, section titles), always ALL CAPS.
- **Barlow ExtraBold** — slide titles, key takeaways, big ideas. Sentence case.
- **Barlow Light, ALL CAPS** — eyebrows, labels, tags, data viz labels.
- **Barlow Regular (or Medium)** — body copy. Sentence case. Keep size hierarchy tight; use the extremes only when needed.

**Accent typeface: Kalam** (hand-drawn/script style). Used for contextual asides, "friendly" callouts, or to highlight data/numbers playfully. **Rules:** always small, always separate from the main text block. **Don't** use Kalam as a header, don't mix Kalam and Barlow inside the same line, don't use Kalam for primary communication.

**Web typefaces** (for web-destined content only): Roboto, Roboto Condensed. Not relevant for most client decks but noted here for completeness.

**Sizes (from the template's type guide):**
- Display: 45 / 30 / 24 pt
- Headlines: 30 / 24 / 18 / 16 / 14 pt
- Labels (ALL CAPS): 16 / 14 / 12 / 10 / 8 pt
- Body: 16 / 10 / 9 / 8 pt

The template's slide layouts handle this automatically — don't manually restyle.

## Logo rules

The Code3 logo lockup has two parts: **monogram** (the C+3 symbol) and **wordmark** ("CODE3").

- **Lockup usage:** black or white only. Never color.
- **Monogram alone:** OK when space is tight (avatars, favicons). The monogram can be used in a single color, or "one-color open" / "one-color closed." Never two-color with the wordmark.
- **Wordmark alone:** NEVER. The wordmark must always accompany the monogram.
- **Two-color monogram:** never combine with the wordmark.
- **Clear space:** minimum clear space = width of the top bar of the "3."
- **Alternate stacked lockup:** available for square-ish spaces.

The template's header/footer areas already place the logo correctly. Don't add, move, or restyle the logo on template slides.

## Visual elements / motifs

From the brand book:

- **Sketch ovals** — stacked ovals for titles/highlighting key content; single oval for a subtle background element.
- **Pill shapes** — used as containers for content or as a hero shape for title cards and dividers.
- **Sketch arrows** — show information flow, call-outs.
- **Doodles** — playful accents (use sparingly; the template includes specific ones).
- **Iconography** — linear, stroked-shape icons. Black OR with a yellow/green highlight on one part. Don't substitute flat/filled icons.

If a slide in the template uses these motifs, they're part of the design — don't remove them. If you need a graphic element not in the template, see if the concept can be expressed without adding one.

## Photography

- **People-first whenever possible.** Active moments.
- **Avoid:** cold/stagnant, staged, stock-looking.
- **B&W vs. color:** B&W for higher-level top-line executions (web hero, abstract). Full color when true-form matters (product shots, team, portraits in action).

The template comes with photography choices already made for each slide that features an image. Don't swap unless the user provides specific imagery.

## Company identity / manifesto themes

Code3 positions itself as: **"Connections + Creative + Commerce — that's our DNA."** Recurring phrases and framings you'll see in real decks:

- "Unlock the code for your brand's growth" (brand tagline)
- "We give more than a damn about our clients"
- "Taking your growth personally"
- "Winning doesn't happen by accident"
- "We don't follow best practices, we create them"
- "Energized by your challenges"
- "Planning and activating as an integrated force"

Don't paste these verbatim into a deck (feels like the manifesto), but when writing company-intro copy, reach for the underlying ideas: integration (across channels), relentlessness, personal ownership, expertise with swagger.

## Quick don'ts

- Don't use "AI-tell" phrases: "delve," "leverage," "navigate the complexities of," "unlock the potential," "landscape," "ecosystem" (unless about actual ecosystems), "embark on," "journey."
- Don't use em dashes heavily — they're OK sparingly but are another AI-tell.
- Don't write in the passive voice ("it was determined that…").
- Don't start slides with filler ("In this section we will discuss…").
- Don't over-qualify ("we believe," "it seems," "in our opinion" — unless a real hedge is needed).
- Don't use stock-corporate words: "synergize," "ideate," "deep dive" (noun), "learnings" is OK (common in Code3 decks), "unpack" is borderline.
- Don't write titles like "A Look at X" or "Understanding X." Write titles that assert something or pose a sharp question.

## Hot takes are human

Code3's voice includes opinion — "our POV is…", a sharp take, a recommendation stated with conviction. But the *specific* hot take on a client's business is a strategic judgment that should come from a Code3 strategist, not be invented by the model. When a slide's punch line is an opinion or a strategic call (not a fact from the data), leave it for a human.

- Mark the slot with `[CALLOUT: human POV needed]` in the slide, and call it out in the delivery note.
- Provide the structure and the supporting facts around it; leave the actual take for the account/strategy lead.
- This keeps the model from fabricating confident opinions Code3 hasn't actually formed, while preserving the space for them in the layout.

Example: a "Looking Ahead" slide can lay out three data-backed options; the recommended bet among them is `[CALLOUT: human POV needed]`.

(This convention is new and not yet battle-tested — watch whether it surfaces meaningfully in real generate-mode outputs or just becomes noise.)

## Punctuation and grammar conventions

- **Oxford comma: use it.** "Creative, Connections, and Commerce" — serial comma before the final conjunction, always.
- **Companies are "it," not "they."** Code3's brand guide treats a company as a singular entity ("Amlactin grew its share," not "their share"). *Caveat: modern usage trends toward "they," and this convention is being confirmed with Jen Frates / Amanda Ferrante. Treat it as a soft default — follow it, but don't aggressively rewrite a client's own usage, and flag if a client clearly prefers "they."*
- Sentence case for content-slide titles; the template handles display and ALL-CAPS casing (see Typography).
